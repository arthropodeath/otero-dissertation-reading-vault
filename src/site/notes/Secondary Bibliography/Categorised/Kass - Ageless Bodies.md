 to their seductive promises of a perfect, better-than-human future, in which we shall all be as gods, ageless and blissful (Kass 9)

Anticipating possible threats to our security, freedom, and even our very humanity, many people are increasingly worried about where biotechnology may be taking us. (Kass 9)**

the intrinsic threat of dehumanization (or the promise of super- humanization). (Kass 10)

not the old crude power to kill the creature made in God's image, but the new science-based power to remake him after our own fantasies. (Kass 10)

Even the bizarre prospect of machine-brain interaction and implanted nanotechnological devices starts with therapeutic efforts to enable the blind to see and the deaf to hear. (Kass 11)**

losing the human import and significance of the undertakings. Better to begin with the likely ends that these powers and techniques are destined to serve: ageless bodies, happy souls, better children, a more peaceful and cooperative society. (Kass 11)

the distinction between healing and enhancing is of limited ethical or practical value. (Kass 13)

This is indeed one of the central arguments against human cloning: the charge of genetic despotism of one generation over the next. (Kass 16)

>there are in fact many human goods that are inseparable from our aging bodies, from our living in time, and from the natural human life cycle by which each generation gives way to the one that follows it (Kass 25)**

>a world of longevity is increasingly a world hostile to children. (Kass 26)
