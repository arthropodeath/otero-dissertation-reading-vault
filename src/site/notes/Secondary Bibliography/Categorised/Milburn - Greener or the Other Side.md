Milburn, Colin, ‘Greener on the Other Side: Science Fiction and the Problem of Green Nanotechnology,’ Configurations, 20.1/2 (2012), 53–87. https://doi.org/10.1353/con.2012.0008.

The opening gambit here—a disavowal of science fiction—aims to firmly separate green nanotechnology from promissory narratives and speculative visions. It reflects a widespread sentiment that the greening of nanotechnology, which is to say, the tremulous aspiration of nanotechnology researchers and companies to “go green,” depends on evacuating speculation and futurism from the industry entirely, concentrating instead on economic realities and environmental impacts in the here and now Milburn 54

green nanotechnology would be a precise mechanism for attenuating risk, minimizing worrisome hazards for both humans and the environment by creating a closed economy of production and consumption, where there are few if any unknowns, few if any gaps Milburn 54

it would 55 leave no cause for speculation, no cause for concern, and certainly no cause for science fiction. Milburn 54-55

green nanotechnology is now aligned with an insidious presentism, which destines the field (and I mean destines in the sense of Heidegger) to an inadvertently reactive position, a conservatism expressed less in its reparatory and remedial efforts than in its acceptance of present social conditions as the only conceivable basis for greening the planet Milburn 56

the extent to which green nanotechnology forecloses science fiction from its discourse is the extent to which it forecloses its own potential for greenness. Milburn 56

science fiction is a privileged cultural form for making a green future thinkable. Robinson’s epic trilogies—in particular, the Mars trilogy and the Science in the Capitol trilogy—represent the greening of nano and other transformative technologies as a function of rebuilding scientific governance from the bottom up Milburn 56

what is most clear is that this so-called green nano vision has little regard for anything beyond human presence—the outside, the nonhuman. Milburn 68

The promotional discourse of green nanotechnology here conjures the same vision of the future as the one featured in the Prius ad: a greening of technology for a world in which green no longer has much to do with preservation of the nonhuman environment, but that instead conceives the human population as the environment. A world where green refers to human survivability and proliferation on the planet, where the human is the last measure of green on Earth—as Toyota has made perfectly clear. Milburn 68

This green nano vision ignores serious thought about very longterm consequences and possible futures, even the possibility of the future being at all different from the present. It would begin to seem that green nanotechnology might not be so green after all. Milburn 68

But let us turn to Robinson’s trilogy of Mars novels—Red Mars (1993), Green Mars (1995), and Blue Mars (1997)—which, among their many remarkable qualities, vividly critique the presentism that 70 would destine nanotechnology and other scientific innovations to a future that is no future, thus limiting a sustainable inhabitation of the planet Milburn 69-70

Some of the experiments are gigantic, such as the NSF project to dump millions of tons of salt into the ocean to restart the stalled Gulf Stream. Others are miniscule, such as Small Delivery System’s genetic engineering of lichen to enhance the carbondrawdown capacity of the Siberian forest (an experiment enmeshed in the semiotics of nanotech, signaled by a nervous joke that the lichen might catastrophically self-replicate: “Maybe instead of gray goo, we die by green goo, eh?”) 81 51. Robinson, Sixty Days and Counting (above, n. 44), p. Milburn 166

what is crucial about these fictive considerations of nano and the politics of green are the formal dispositions of science fiction as such, the narrative mechanisms for articulating scientific and social change together as co-constitutive and irreducible. In this regard, science fiction remains a privileged mode for considering radical difference: different sociopolitical orders, different bio-ecological orders, different ontological orders Milburn 87
