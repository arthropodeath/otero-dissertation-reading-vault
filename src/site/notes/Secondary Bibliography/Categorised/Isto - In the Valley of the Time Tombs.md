The monument is frequently presented as belonging to another place and another time, reflecting an image of reality that is at once reminiscent of our own and perpetually inscrutable. The monument’s displacement in time is importantly related to the aesthetic of the ruin: either the monument appears eroded by time and swallowed by obscurity, or else it paradoxically presents itself as eternally preserved, reflecting no sign of age despite its implied antiquity Isto 491

>Despite their clear association with the past, monuments are often anticipatory—they gesture toward the future. Isto  491

>the treatment of monuments as paradigmatic sf forms that relate to the genre’s broader concern with temporal multi-directionality, futurity, and questions of historical inevitability Isto  492

The example of John Taine’s *The Time Stream* already stretches across several of these paradigms. The monuments populating the desert that Herron visits are at once a reflection of historical knowledge and an effort to transform the future by means of that knowledge; they allow Herron not only to learn of specific scientific discoveries, but also to transform how he perceives time itself. Isto 493
