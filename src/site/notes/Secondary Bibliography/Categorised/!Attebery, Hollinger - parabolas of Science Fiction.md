Though there are several words for this phenomenon already in circulation—such as trope, subgenre, and theme—parabola, with its echoes of orbits and equations, not only matches the generic decor but also more suggestively describes the way certain shared narrative patterns integrate narrative needs, scientific information, and metacommentary on the genre itself. 18

The genre is similarly dismissed by literary snobs and relegated to special sections of the bookstore or book review. Sf shares a pulp magazine history (and a fair number of writers) with men’s adventure and tough-guy detective stories. The same commercial forces that encourage filmmakers to study and imitate features from successful movies—the genre-building process that Rick Altman calls the “producer’s game” (1999, 38)—operate in written sf 19

One important piece of this history is the deliberate invention of sf fandom. Gernsback and Campbell both included reader responses in the form of letters columns in their magazines, and both encouraged readers to meet together in clubs and conventions to which writers and editors were invited as guests.  20

Because these two histories intertwine, sf has never been either a purely literary or a purely popular genre. It has multiple readerships, and its readers often read across genre and canonical lines. 20

For readers who can and do invoke the sf megatext, it serves not only to enrich the text but also to affiliate the story with science itself 26

It can carry the familiar out into a universe of strangeness, or it can bring alien beings and practices from outer space down to the Earth. Every writer who invokes the icon must choose whether to emphasize phallic power or maternal shelter, frontier individuality or corporate power, and lonely pilots or teeming cities in space 27

The sf megatext holds many such trajectories: the postholocaust story, the superman story, the first-contact story, the alternate history, and the rediscovery of a lost-colony world 27

Having evoked a parabola such as the generation starship, a writer can rely on readers to supply information from the megatext 28

A parabola is not only a mathematical concept but also the root word of parable. Sf is parabolic in both senses: an open-ended curve and a vehicle for significance. By invoking one of the genre’s familiar story forms, a writer invites the reader to look for implications outside the world of the story. Just as some images are more likely than others to develop into icons, some forms of stories are especially apt for serving as parables. It is significant that sf’s parabolas are often referred to by writers and critics as tropes, a term that implies not only conventionality but also figuration: a voyage lasting for lifetimes; a ship that is also a world; worldmakers who are not gods but engineers; an environment in which limits are absolute and evident to the senses; and a space that inverts the concepts of inside and outside, up and down—any and all of these aspects of the scenario can figure forth insights into the world of the reader’s experience 30

One of the most important functions of the parabola is to allow stories to function both figuratively and literally, as metaphoric representations of and convincing extrapolations from current ideas and practices, without collapsing into one reading or the other 34

science fictional parabola is a collective enterprise, like the formulas of other popular genres and of oral forms such as the fairy tale. Unlike those formulas it invites new additions and corrections. Each use of the generation-starship scenario, for instance, builds and comments upon earlier uses. Second, as a parabola evolves, it allows writers to unload some of the text’s burdens. The existence of the parabola allows writers to pare away large chunks of exposition and focus on images and actions relevant to their chosen themes. It also enriches the text by providing links to the scientific and generic megatext. The more of that megatext the reader can bring to the story, the more complex and subtle it will seem. Third, the parabola offers a starting point with no obligatory conclusion. It sets the scene, offers a range of characters and incidents, and then leaves the writer relatively free. Like the starship itself, the story can reach its implied destination or venture into the unknown. If it arrives at a new planet, the characters can decide to stay in orbit or swing their craft onward in a new direction. But whatever their decision, the parabolic structure of the story invites the reader to find applications outside the text. 38

What is unusual about sf is not its capacity for change or creative disruption—even the most formulaic versions of romance or detective story can incorporate new and surprising variations. Such variations are welcomed so long as they do not change the fundamental rules of the game. However, sf is distinctive among popular genres in that no degree of mode stretching is enough to throw a work out of category. Indeed, the readers’ hunger for new ideas and ways of thinking about and living within those ideas demands that the story structures themselves keep changing. 39
