McCarron, Kevin. “Corpses, Animals, Machines and Mannequins: The Body and Cyberpunk.” Cyberspace/Cyberbodies/Cyberpunk: Cultures of Technological Embodiment, Featherstone, Mike and Burrows, Roger (eds.). SAGE Publications Ltd, 1995, pp. 261–74, [https://doi.org/10.4135/9781446250198.n15](https://doi.org/10.4135/9781446250198.n15).

Cited in [[Chapters/2- Mortal, Matronly and Made from Flesh, The Fear of the Excessive Body in Bruce Sterling’s Schismatrix\|2- Mortal, Matronly and Made from Flesh, The Fear of the Excessive Body in Bruce Sterling’s Schismatrix]]

About [[Terminology/Embodiment\|Embodiment]], [[Terminology/Cyberpunk\|Cyberpunk]], [[Terminology/Criticism of Extropianism\|Criticism of Extropianism]]

>the 'body of texts, hterary and cinematic, which comprises the genre of cyberpunk constitutes a sustained meditation, unrivalled in contemporary culture, on the Cartesian mind/body dichotomy. (McCarron 261)**

>the body, which features in cyberpunk not at all as a biological essence, a little more as a site for cultural enscription, but most of all as an encumbrance, dragging the mind back from the disembodied purity of cyberspatial interaction with the matrix: (McCarron 261)

perhaps paradoxically, much of cyberpunk's appeal lies in its Puritanical dismissal of the body (McCarron 262)

The body, for cyberpunk writers, is an 'accident', unconnected to the pure substance of mind. They are fascinated by 'enhancement'; throughout their novels the human body becomes less organic and more artificial (McCarron 262)**

When Descartes asserts the division between mind and body he necessarily accepts a further dichotomy, this time between humans and animals (McCarron 263)**

Cyperpunk writers introduce machinery in the form of 'enhanced humans, androids and cyborgs which complicates Descartes satisfying binary division (McCarron 263)

Whenever the textual stress is on prosthetics, on the kind of technology that fuses blood and iron, the debate is really about the human, and its sometimes opposite, the inhuman (McCarron 264)**

The computer is important to cyberpunk; indeed its very existence is a generic constant. However, it is not only as hardware that the computer is important. (McCarron 266)

we are our memories, but if these can be implanted what guarantee is there of our identity? Or, more existentially perhaps, always assuming identity and existence are separate phenomena, what guarantee is there of our existence? (McCarron 266)

the end of private memory, a feature of our humanity (McCarron 267)

>Running through all cyberpunk texts is a fascination with the ways in which the flesh is inessential, irrelevant; there is a disdain for the too, too human flesh (McCarron 267)**

>such things as prosthetic devices and lens implants indicates not symbolic corruption but consumer desire, hnked to the great American belief in reinventing the self. (McCarron 268)

>Despite the array of traditionally masculine plot lines and images which appear throughout cyberpunk; leather, drugs, violence, crime, technology, etc, the really macho aspect of cyberpunk lies in its complete lack of interest in biological reproduction (McCarron 270)

The Corporation is, in a sense, the ultimate body (McCarron 271)

In the 'face of increasing mechanization, cyberpunk's Cartesian privileging of mind allows its readers to reassert their supremacy over the machine. (McCarron 272)

cyberpunk narratives suggest that machines may have minds, but human beings are minds (McCarron 272)
