>SF alone in Anglo-Saxon literature has retained the wilderness as a viable, constant preoccupation for a general, intelligent reading audience. SF's wild planetary terrains of forests, oceans, deserts, and mountains, and the inter- planetary regions of outer space, have kept alive the wilderness geography of physical challenge, emotional wonder, spiritual hope, and the exploration of the unknown Dean 68

The highly Americanized, modern form of SF preserves American ideals of wilderness. SF can nicely accomplish this feat because it can rewrite history: it builds stories on the basis of altered, hypothetical states rather than blunt, empirical reality. Wilderness is lost-but wilderness is not lost. SF provides us with new, green worlds for old Dean 69

>The conventions of SF invite the use of wilderness. The infinite, timeless realms of SF are provocative wilderness landscapes. They are adversaries. They are gauges. They are places of immediate, intuitive self- knowledge where one rises or falls, triumphs or dies. SF heroes can eventually go so far in their wanderings that all frontiers are negated. Where are boundaries in deep space? What frontiers exist in a cosmic void? Dean 70

. Humanity faces an aggressive, Earth-devouring forest planted by an alien intelligence which is converting Earth into one vast farm and erradicating mankind as if men were no more than worms. This forest is referred to as the monolithic "Plant" or "Plants." The Plants are like sequoias, the oldest living species of trees native to the Northern Hemisphere. They are extrapo- lations on the ancient baobab tree of Africa and India-second only to the sequoia in trunk diameter -upon the sap of which man may feed. The Plants especially recall the primal forest which covered North America until the onslaught of European man. But in The Genocides the civilizing process of clearing the forest is being reversed with a vengeance Dean 72
