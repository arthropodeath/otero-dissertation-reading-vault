Regardless of the era in which scholars, the media, and others write about monsters, there is always an emphasis on their rise and their return, as if they increase and multiply unbeknown to unsuspecting witnesses. (McCormack 250

**Whatever form monsters take, they endure as cultural, social, ethical, and political material/ representational manifestations of that which challenges – or threatens to dismantle – the normative structures of a supposedly human life on a planet shared with human and non/in/ahuman others. (McCormack 250**

**the monsters of critical posthumanism bring to the fore the uncertainty of the category of the human, the dangers and playfulness of defying humanness, as well addressing what is at stake in a historically exclusive category that facilitates privilege, violence, and centuries of oppression. To hold onto the category of the human as distinct from the animal, as forged through white privilege and oppression, and as a hierarchical evolutionary position is to reinforce a heteropatriarchal, racist structure (McCormack 250**

The monster may evoke ideas of difference, otherness, and a being or thing from over there. However, the monster brings those connotations much closer to home and self, often showing how such conceptions of embodied being and belonging are founded through that which is seen as exterior, inferior, and needing to be expulsed (McCormack 250

**While the process of monstering may try to distance self from other, supposed evil other from innocent self, colonizer from colonized, it also reveals a proximity that may unsettle, disturb, and even threaten. (McCormack 251**

**Critical posthumanism, with its emphasis on the undoing of the human in its delineated, closed, and autonomous forms, has been and continues to be a framework for thinking and interpreting the significance, meanings, and permutations of the monster (McCormack 252**

the monster occupies a familiar posthumanist position of interrogating on the one hand the meaning of the human, including what beings and embodiments fall outside such a position, and on the other hand what has come to be positioned on the outside or the margins (McCormack 252

**The monster is that which is not the normative human: that which is rejected from the norm in often indefinable and fluctuating forms given shape through racism, ableism, sexism, transphobia, homophobia, classism, and other hierarchical vectors of power (McCormack 254

**the monster comes to embody that which the norm is not. It is invested with the anxieties, prejudices, and violence of any given time, where some aspects may shift and change and others may appear longstanding (e.g., racism, transphobia, homophobia, classism, ableism as projected onto and constituted by the imagining of monstrous beings). (McCormack 256

**to become human is part of the colonial state apparatus that disempowers those fighting for a less violent and still to be imagined society. (McCormack 259

Thus when thinking the monster, it is not enough to argue for a more elastic version of the human or to suggest the monster undoes the human and thereby makes it appealing or available as a position to occupy. Rather, we witness the monster as the willful act of defiance against the systemic injustices of colonialism, racism, transphobia, homophobia, ableism, sexism, as well as the attempt to imagine otherwise (McCormack 260

the plasticity of the monster is its capacity to be everything without being any one thing: it can be about race, sexuality, gender, ability, physical difference, specific incarnations of animality and hybrid beings, acts of violence, historical and ongoing genocides, and so much more that I fail to catch in this list. Such plasticity means it is always in play. It is not simply what is other or different, but it may *define* otherness and difference. The monster also structures what is animal, as well as what is human; how race and disability are animalized; how race, sexuality, gender, ability, and class are read and projected on to human and nonhuman animals, and how sex, race, gender, community living/belonging, and ability are interpreted through non/a/in/humans; what is animate and inanimate (including dead and alive); and how we understand such definitions and remember what is excluded, destroyed, denied, or hidden (McCormack 268
