Englender, Yonatan. ‘Dreams of Sheep: Humanity as a Discursive Formation in Battlestar Galactica’. _The Uncertain Future of Empathy : Posthumanism, Cyberculture and Science Fiction_, edited by Elsa Bouet, 1st edn, Inter-Disciplinary Press, 2015, pp. 67–74.

About [[Terminology/Posthumanism\|Posthumanism]], [[Terminology/AI\|AI]]

Post-humanism, however, is not as concerned with artificial creations replacing humans as it is with humanity's behaviour in a world which is increasingly technological (Englender 68

**Facing an artificial creation that is capable of reason, emotion, and even death, surely the very notion of ‘human’ becomes questionable, unsustainable, even obsolete. (Englender 69**

**The fear, then, is not of losing one’s human ‘essence’, but rather of losing the possibility of being defined as human. Put differently, humanity’s greatest fear is discovering that the term ‘human’ is no longer relevant. (Englender 70**

**The question is, then, whether a text that is created within a humanist tradition and framework can conceive a post-human entity that isn’t tinged with human ideology. More simply put, can we imagine a robot that does not have human values and aspirations? (Englender 71**

>Do no other alternatives exist? Must android mentality mimic human mentality? Can we not conceive of machines which will choose for themselves goals other than the ones we as humans chose for ourselves? (Englender 72

>we will arrive at the conclusion that as post-human discourse has one leg dragged behind it in human discourse, a posthuman creation will inevitably have in it a grain of humanity. In the same way that we create art that corresponds and reflects our human mentality and values, we will have no alternative but to create machines which reflect the same (Englender 72
