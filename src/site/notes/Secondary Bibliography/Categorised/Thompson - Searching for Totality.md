Thompson, Craig. “Searching for Totality: Antinomy and the ‘Absolute’ in Bruce Sterling’s ‘Schismatrix’ (A La Recherche de La Totalité: Antinomie et ‘l’Absolu’ Dans ‘Schismatrix’ de Bruce Sterling).” Science-Fiction Studies, vol. 18, no. 2, 1991, pp. 198–209.

About [[Primary Bibliography/2. Sterling/1. Schismatrix Plus\|1. Schismatrix Plus]]

Abelard Lindsay is transported, protected, contained, restrained and mediated by vessels that create interiors and exteriors (Thompson 198)

He escapes from the vessel of language and finds himself placed in the world of infinite chaos- excluded from the finite and unable to see those things, such as selfhood, that depend on limits (Thompson 198)

Neither group sees him as one of their own- the Mechanists do not trust him because he has received Shaper training, but true Shapers think this rudimentary training has left him an incondite simulation as well (Thompson 199)

>Preserving and celebrating the trait they have in common- mortality. (Thompson 199)

>This aquatic world comes as close as a material world can to providing complete integration between its inhabitants and their environment (Thompson 199)

Lindsay’s embrace of the polymorphous entity is only the final act in his evolving, lifelong battle against vessels. (Thompson 199)

The worlds in Schismatrix are shells which the characters live within, not solid masses that provide life to those on the surfaces. (Thompson 200)

A ship designed as a world, or a world designed as a ship (Thompson 200)

The vessels further fragment realities; the worlds in which the characters live make it even more difficult to know a whole universe (Thompson 200)

When the design of the body becomes a motive for power struggles, new barriers are created that further alienate humans from each other (Thompson 200)

The word is a vessel that permits only a certain range of thought (Thompson 201)

>Lindsay’s most portentous effort at overcoming boundaries is represented by his construction of Europa (Thompson 204)

Nothing is more obscene than death, particularly when brought on purposely (Thompson 205)

>When Lindsay breaks an “Interdict” against travelling to Earth- the origin of humanity in the solar system- he sees a world that has tried to remain unchanged, and the necessity of progress is driven home (Thompson 205)
