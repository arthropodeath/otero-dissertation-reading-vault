DiTommaso, L. (2007) The Articulation of Imperial Decadence and Decline in Epic Science Fiction. _Extrapolation_. [Online] 48 (2), 267–291.

About [[Terminology/Science Fiction Studies\|Science Fiction Studies]], [[Terminology/Utopia\|Utopia]]

>the theme of decline is expressed primarily by means of the language of decadence and degeneration, whose vocabulary is principally cultural, economic, and social DiTommaso 269

>The idea that a self-absorbed, stagnant population is symbolic of the decadence that hallmarks decline DiTommaso 273

>A lack of curiosity, particularly regarding technical and scientific matters, also connotes a lack of vitality on the part of a stagnant and decadent population DiTommaso 274

>Science and religion are understood as opposites, and the passing of the former is employed as a clear signal of the increasing decline of the population DiTommaso 277

decadence is understood to be both a distinguishing mark and a principal cause of a state or empire in decline ... the social structure and value system of a declining population is linked with its stagnation DiTommaso 279

This articulation of the dynamic of decline reflects a historical perspective that understands empires or other corporate structures (such as states, races, or even the species) organically DiTommaso 279

>the capacity for self-denial, which is understood to rest on an innate sense of righteousness critical to any corporation that seeks to transcend the sum of its constituent members, and whose lack is characteristic of any society whose members are willing to sacrifice the common weal for base personal gain DiTommaso 280

>In general, these heroes are marked by vitality, industry, frontier self-sufficiency, democratic equality, and, to be fair, a dose of healthy capitalism. I wonder whether these attributes and the combination of elightened self-interest and respect for society's structures is no more than a reworking of the classic American ethos, real or romanticized, from the period before the 1960s DiTommaso 283
