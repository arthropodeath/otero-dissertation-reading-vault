A call for historical awareness from critics of science fiction, a demand that the critic not assume, simply because contemporary science fiction is a young mode, that its history begins when the critic began reading it. There are many incidents and influences between 1926 and the present that in concert make up the “history” of contemporary science fiction Delany wine 240

Science fiction takes place on the margins of that history, sometimes influenced by it, sometimes in reaction against it, but more often simply outside it Delany wine 240

A demand to keep narrative/aesthetic forms (space opera, single-new-invention stories, parallel-world stories, social-alternative tales, etc.) distinct from aesthetic quality (well written, badly written, richly imagined, poorly imagined, schematic, incoherent, highly inventive, psychologically or sociologically involuted, etc.). By the same token, these two areas of judgment must be kept separate from a third, the value systems that control the text (the various ideological reductions that can be made by asking certain questions of the narrative structure or of various metaphorical systems: what social classes benefit from these ideas? what social classes are oppressed by them? etc.) Delany wine 240

The assumption of unilateral crosscategory associations (“all space operas necessarily express only one value system”; “only social-alternative stories can necessarily be of high aesthetic quality”) must be avoided. Within such unilateral associative assumptions lie the end of analysis and the beginning of critical terrorism, something to be avoided in all areas of criticism but far more pernicious in a young field still feeling its way. Delany wine 240

A practice of writing, like science fiction, is the collection of codes used to make texts said to belong to it “make sense” (or “yield meaning”). But any single code exists as a response to historical pressures (more accurately, to socio-psychological attractions) in the real world. Discourse happens, and happens in response to the world: it does not merely exist in some timeless and innocent space of abstract language possibilities. Delany wine 241

What contemporary science fiction did was organize a set of reading protocols that could make (essentially technological) sense out of a whole set of sentences that would have been simply incomprehensible to both Verne and the young Wells Delany wine 242

By the bye, my beef with Darko Suvin’s concept of the “novum” is not that it doesn’t work or isn’t true. Rather, my disagreement is purely methodological. (I should strike that “purely,” of course. In this world, nothing is pure.) The novum is not clearly embodied in a textual unit. I’m sure you can imagine an SF text, however trivial the case may seem, in which the novum was nowhere mentioned explicitly, but only implied. The novum is a reduction one makes of the “mental movie” (the diegesis) the text produces. If the text were only a “play of presences,” then you could always locate the novum with a particular verbal unit. But since the text is a “play of presences and absences,” you can get into trouble with such a reductive method. Delany wine 243

Science fiction is much reread. But it has not developed its own critical models for the rereading process Delany wine 245

An ideological reduction is made of a narrative text by asking certain questions about the narrative (Who succeeds? Who fails? What social class would benefit by this failure? What social class would be oppressed by this failure? etc.) or, by extension, asking certain questions about various metaphorical systems used to present the narrative. Thus every text has an ideological reduction. There is no escaping ideology, in that sense. By asking a similar set of questions one can make an ideological reduction of a critical text as well. Ideological abuse arises from assuming either that the particular questions by which one makes an ideological reduction are the only questions to ask, or that the ideological reduction determines the ultimate worth of the text, or that in some way this reduction totalizes the structure of all other meanings that can be read from the text by way of *other* questions that can be asked about it Delany wine 246

My view is that no one can escape ideology: at best, one can be more or less aware of one’s own ideology (or, as the case may be, aware of the ideology reducible from one’s own text, which often is not the ideology one personally subscribes to!); one can only be more or less critical of it. I see the choice, then, as between articulating a more or less considered ideology or expressing an ideology blindly Delany wine 246

If science fiction is not distinct from other categories of writing, if the historical forces that contour the situation of the SF writer differently from that of the literary writer are not significant, if the historically contoured needs that make a reader choose to read an SF text at one moment and a literary text at another are not meaningful, then why are we producing a journal devoted to science fiction in the first place? What this ultimately produces in the SF critical field is an array of terms that discuss only similarities. The field of critical terminology, because it is appropriated, suggests the similarities are much more pervasive than they actually are. Delany wine 256

The difference between science fiction and what I have taken to calling mundane fiction is not the difference we find, within, say, mundane prose fiction, between the Gothic and the psychological; it is not the same as the difference, within poetry, between the sonnet and the sestina; it is not the same as the difference, within drama, between tragedy and comedy. Within science fiction, as within these other categories, there are subcategories that bear such distinctions, such as near-future and farfuture science fiction and the parallel-worlds story. But these subcategories are held together, as it were, by the greater category protocols Delany wine 258

much of the significance of the SF story will manifest itself in the alternative workings of the world in which the characters maneuver. Therefore we will read with special attention to the rhetorical figures by which differences between our world and the world of the story are suggested Delany wine 260

I will not go so far as to say the history of science fiction does not exist. I do, however, think that before any historical inquiry occurs a fundamental process takes place, a process so fundamental we are apt to lose sight of it. Delany wine 262
