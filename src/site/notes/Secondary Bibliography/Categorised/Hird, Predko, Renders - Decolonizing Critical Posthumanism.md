It is within this context that we consider recent claims within the posthumanism oeuvre that western and Indigenous worldviews may be brought together – indeed may be reconciled in some way – and bear on efforts to tackle the planet-wide challenge of living in the Anthropocene. Our research and practice call attention to the limits of this widespread and largely unquestioned effort and its continued (settler) colonial undercurrents. Decolonizing posthumanism, we argue, requires that we acknowledge the limits of incorporating or otherwise “blending” Indigenous and posthumanist ontologies, epistemologies, theories, and praxis. Posthumanism emerged from extended debates within philosophy, discourse studies, and other disciplines about the limits of humanism for thinking through urgent concerns, including planetary changes wrought by the Anthropocene. (Hird 540

And so, whether it is considering what it means to be human after the death of the universalized (white, male, and economically, culturally, and politically privileged) “Man” of Socratic and then Enlightenment philosophy, what it means to share (however grudgingly acknowledged or stubbornly denied) the planet with nonhuman critters, the viability of some surviving notion of species in the face of inorganic planetary forces (such as climate change), or the differential vulnerabilities of the subaltern, at its core, posthumanism seeks to develop a new ethical register out of the ashes, we might say, of humanism. This defines, indeed, humanism’s “post.” (Hird 542
