Burfoot, Annette. ‘Human Remains: Identity Politics in the Face of Biotechnology’. _Cultural Critique_, vol. 53, no. 1, 2003, pp. 47–71., [https://doi.org/10.1353/cul.2003.0019](https://doi.org/10.1353/cul.2003.0019).

I propose a new term, "biopleasure," to appreciate the signification of matter in the context of contemporary technoscientific speculations of bodies as atomized, degendered, and nonhuman. Burfoot 48

posthumanism, especially as formulated in technoscience, can be seen to reify the objectification of the body in terms of denying its formative role and by affirming it as irreducible atomic matter. Biopleasure speaks to the combined and contradictory nature of biotechnology narratives that simultaneously dehumanizes, demonizes, and eroticizes human being. Burfoot 48

I use "biopleasure" to connote a fetishism of the body as components (reproductive cells, uteri, genes, and so on), in which the life-giving qualities of the body are produced in reduced, mechanized, and malleable but mute forms (such as DNA) Burfoot 49

Technologies of the body are not only au courant in identity politics debates, but reproductive technology, genetic engineering, and medical visualization technologies all provide ample material for disputing the nature of reality, matter, and materialization. They also represent profound statements on the value of maternity and reproduction: feminine principles. Burfoot 57

One of the most intense debates of bodily significance and its role as matter can be found in the well-developed area of new reproductive technologies (NRTs). Here bodies have become atomized into reproductive gametes and redrawn as innovative conceptions. Burfoot 61

oMaterialization could also be used to support some feminist claims that NRTs actually liberate women from heterosexist expectations regarding reproduction: perhaps lesbians will soon be able to reproduce with the genetic material from both mothers, and reproduction can now be completely separated from heterosexual intercourse. Burfoot 62

earthly and actual viral threats, such as AIDS and the Ebola and Hantavirus varieties, have entered the scene as well. They, too, provide a version of the compelling Cartesian battle between technoscientific control and alien beings that pose a deadly threat to fragile human bodies. There has been a plethora of popular cultural shows, especially in the form of television journalism, serials, and made-for-television films over the last decade, that treat us to one scene after another of the human body under viral attack Burfoot 66

Reproductive technoscience is the iteration of masculine-as-cognitive (scientific) domination of feminine-as-material (body). The possibility of upsetting that iteration is raised by some who point out, for example, how female doctors also practice these techniques on women Burfoot 67

 Yet none of these counterinteractions deny the visual and other pleasures derived from the practice that rely on the degradation, atomization, or denial of the material body as significant Burfoot 67

Finally, science and medicine in general have considerable histories as materially discursive bodies themselves that have been dominated by masculine interests and by men Burfoot 68

Women's bodies remain the actual and symbolic focus of the attention of reproductive science. The science fiction fantasies and science documentaries discussed here both rely on the abject body as feminine or black and on a concept of reproduction as death Burfoot 68

Finally, providing agency to scientific bodies may be intellectually stimulating, but it also carries political risks. Assigning agency to scientific apparatuses (even though Barad's aim to dismantle observation- independent bodies is a good one) contributes to the atomization of human bodies as a "natural" act, thus obscuring the political motivations for doing so and further denying the body as a significant aspect of cognition.
